How to start email marketing in Nepal

Updated June 2026 · 7 min read

Email is still the highest-ROI channel in marketing — you own your list, no algorithm decides who sees it. Here's how a Nepali business starts from zero, the right way.

Step 1 — Build a list you're allowed to email

The biggest beginner mistake is buying or scraping a list. It tanks your deliverability and it's bad practice. Instead, collect opt-in contacts:

  • A signup form on your website or a hosted landing page.
  • A checkbox at checkout / point of sale.
  • An incentive — a discount, a guide, early access — in exchange for an email.

A small list of people who want your email beats a big list that doesn't.

Step 2 — Pick a tool that fits Nepal

You need a platform that handles the sending, the design, and the deliverability. For a Nepali business, the practical filters are: can I pay in NPR (eSewa/Khalti)?, does it land in Gmail/Outlook?, and is it simple enough to use without a marketer on staff?

Step 3 — Authenticate your domain

Before your first send, set up SPF, DKIM and DMARC and send from your own domain (not @gmail.com). This is what keeps you out of spam — full deliverability guide here. A good tool generates these records for you.

Step 4 — Design your first campaign

Keep it simple: a clear subject line, one main message, one call to action. Use a drag-and-drop editor so you're not writing code — drop a header, some text, a button, done. Make sure it looks right on mobile, where most people read.

Your first email doesn't need to be clever. It needs to reach the inbox, look clean on a phone, and have one obvious next step.

Step 5 — Send, then measure

After you send, watch three numbers:

  • Open rate — is your subject line working? (A healthy range is ~25–45%.)
  • Click rate — is your content compelling?
  • Unsubscribes / spam complaints — keep these low by emailing only opted-in people, at a sensible frequency.

Then repeat. Email marketing compounds: every campaign teaches you what your audience responds to.
